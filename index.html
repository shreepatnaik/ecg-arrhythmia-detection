01 Problem & Approach

Cardiac arrhythmias cause irregular heart rhythms and are a leading cause of sudden cardiac death. While ECG signals are the gold standard for diagnosis, real-world recordings are often contaminated by noise from muscle movement, power line interference, and electrode contact issues. This research evaluates how deep learning models perform under clean conditions versus degraded signal quality, and whether fine-tuning pre-trained models on noisy data can recover performance.

Dataset
MIT-BIH
48 records · 360 Hz · ~110K beats
5-class AAMI standard: Normal, Supraventricular, Ventricular, Fusion, Unknown
Models
LSTM ↔ MLP
Bidirectional LSTM captures temporal dependencies in heartbeat sequences. MLP baseline treats each beat as a flat feature vector. Both evaluated under 3 noise conditions.
Key Hypothesis
Fine-tuning matters
Pre-trained models lose accuracy on noisy ECG. Fine-tuning on noise-augmented data is critical for recovering performance, especially at low SNR.

07 Key Findings
LSTM excels at temporal pattern capture
Bidirectional LSTM achieves 99.0% accuracy on clean data by learning sequential P-QRS-T dependencies that MLP cannot model. The 3.8% accuracy gap widens to 9.9% under low-SNR conditions.
Fine-tuning recovers noise-degraded performance
At 10dB SNR, LSTM accuracy drops from 99.0% to 89.4%. Fine-tuning on noise-augmented data recovers it to 93.7% — a 4.3% improvement that makes deployment viable in noisy clinical environments.
Fusion beats are the hardest to classify
Class F (Fusion) has the lowest per-class F1 across all models due to its hybrid morphology combining ventricular and normal characteristics, compounded by limited training samples.
SNR threshold for clinical viability
Above 20dB SNR, both models maintain clinically acceptable accuracy (>95%). Below 10dB, MLP becomes unreliable while LSTM (especially fine-tuned) remains viable — critical insight for wearable ECG devices.
